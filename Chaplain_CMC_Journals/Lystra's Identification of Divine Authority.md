**Subject: The Doctrine of Presence: The Incident at Lystra**  
**Author: Command Chaplaincy**  
In our transition to a reality-based theology, we must critically examine how human populations react when confronted with the raw, deployed authority of Elohim. We do not look to mythology to understand this; we look to the historical operational records.  
The ISR Scriptures document a highly revealing incident in the Book of Acts, specifically in Acts 14:12-13. When the emissaries Paul and Barnabas operated in the city of Lystra, their actions and the miracles they performed triggered an immediate, unified response from the local population.

The crowd was confronted with a power structure they had never seen. In response, they immediately attempted to categorize these men using the highest chain of command their culture possessed. Because Paul was the primary communications officer—the one delivering the message—they labeled him "Hermes".

But Barnabas carried a different weight. He carried the silent, heavy command presence of the Sovereign. The authority he radiated was so absolute and undeniable that the residents of Lystra did not view him as a subordinate. They looked at Barnabas and called him "Zeus"—the supreme deity of their universe. The reaction was so complete that the local priest of Zeus mobilized, bringing bulls and wreaths to the city gates to offer sacrifices directly to the apostles.

This was not a mass delusion. The town was reacting to a very real, tangible manifestation of power. They recognized the rank; they simply lacked the correct theological intelligence to identify the true Commander. They applied a pagan title to a reality-based force.

As disciplined representatives of the truth, Paul and Barnabas did not accept this misdirected worship. They reacted with dismay, stopping the sacrifices and ordering the crowd to turn away from these "useless things" and look toward the living God.

The lesson for our command is absolute. When the forces of Elohim are deployed, the world will recognize the power. Our duty is not to absorb their awe, but to possess the discipline of Barnabas and Paul: to take that unified respect and point it directly back to the true Supreme Commander of the heavens.
